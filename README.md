The Romanov family was the last imperial dynasty of Russia and ruled the country for over 300 years, between 1613 and 1917. The Romanovs came to power after a period of crisis known as the "Time of Troubles," when Russia faced famine, wars, and disputes over the throne. In 1613, Michael Romanov was chosen as Tsar, initiating a new phase for the Russian Empire.

Over the centuries, the Romanovs greatly expanded Russia's territory, transforming the country into one of the world's greatest powers. Among the dynasty's best-known rulers are Peter the Great, who modernized the army and brought Russia closer to European culture, and Catherine the Great, who further strengthened the empire and encouraged the arts and education.

At the beginning of the 20th century, Russia was experiencing major social and economic problems. The population suffered from poverty, inequality, and lack of rights, while the imperial family maintained a luxurious lifestyle. The last Tsar, Nicholas II, faced strong opposition, exacerbated by defeat in World War I and the country's internal crises.

In 1917, the Russian Revolution occurred, overthrowing the monarchy and ending the Romanov rule. Nicholas II abdicated the throne, and the imperial family was imprisoned by the Bolshevik revolutionaries. In July 1918, Nicholas II, his wife Alexandra, and their children were executed in Yekaterinburg, marking the definitive end of the Romanov dynasty.

Even after their fall, the Romanovs remained one of the most well-known royal families in history. Their trajectory is remembered both for the enormous power they wielded and for their tragic end during the Russian Revolution.
